## What the Act is

The **Safety, Health and Welfare at Work Act 2005** is the primary piece of Irish workplace health and safety legislation. It consolidated and replaced the 1989 Act and sets out the general duties employers owe to their employees. The [DSE Regulations](/irish-hs-reference/dse-regulations) (S.I. No. 299 of 2007) sit underneath this Act as a specific set of rules for display screen work.

The full text is available on the [Irish Statute Book](https://www.irishstatutebook.ie/eli/2005/act/10/enacted/en/print.html).

## The general duty of care

The core obligation for employers is found in **Section 8** of the Act. In plain English, an employer must, so far as is reasonably practicable:

- Manage and conduct work in a way that ensures the safety, health and welfare of employees
- Design, provide and maintain a safe place of work and safe means of access and egress
- Provide safe systems of work, plant, machinery and equipment
- Provide information, instruction, training and supervision
- Carry out a written risk assessment and prepare a safety statement
- Consult with employees on matters of safety, health and welfare

The phrase **"so far as is reasonably practicable"** is important — the Act recognises that cost, time and technical difficulty can limit what an employer can reasonably do, but the bar is high and the burden of showing something wasn't reasonably practicable falls on the employer.

## How Usafety helps with the general duty

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Risk assessment (Section 19)" icon="magnifying-glass">
    Section 19 requires every employer to carry out a risk assessment of the workplace and keep it up to date. For DSE workstations, the Usafety assessment is a full risk assessment — it identifies hazards (chair, desk, monitor, lighting, posture), evaluates them, and records the findings.

    The [compliance dashboard](/equipment-and-compliance/compliance-dashboard) gives you the organisation-wide view of who's been assessed and who hasn't.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Safety statement (Section 20)" icon="file-contract">
    Section 20 requires employers to prepare a written safety statement that specifies how safety, health and welfare will be secured. Your Usafety records — assessments, equipment fulfilment, remediation status — form part of the evidence base for your safety statement's DSE section.

    Export bulk PDF reports when you need to append concrete data to your statement. See [Exporting equipment reports](/equipment-and-compliance/exporting-equipment-reports).
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Employee consultation (Section 26)" icon="comments">
    Section 26 requires consultation with employees on matters likely to affect their safety, health or welfare. The Usafety assessment flow *is* the consultation mechanism — employees self-report issues, upload photos of their workstation and explain what they need.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Training (Section 10)" icon="graduation-cap">
    Section 10 requires employers to ensure employees receive appropriate training and instruction. The assessment walkthrough plus the assessor's feedback serves as practical DSE training.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Accommodation and adjustments" icon="wheelchair">
    The general duty of care encompasses reasonable accommodations for employees with disabilities, pregnant employees and those returning from injury. Usafety's per-assessment recommendation and comment threads let you record what's been offered, approved or declined as part of an accommodation discussion.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Penalties for non-compliance

The Act gives the HSA significant enforcement powers. Breaches can lead to:

- Improvement notices (requiring you to fix something within a set period)
- Prohibition notices (stopping a dangerous activity immediately)
- Summary prosecution (fines up to €3,000 per offence)
- Indictable prosecution (unlimited fines and up to two years imprisonment for serious breaches)

In practice, the HSA uses its enforcement powers proportionately, and most DSE-related issues are resolved with improvement notices.

## Record keeping

The Act doesn't specify a minimum retention period for safety records, but common practice is to keep them for **at least seven years** — aligned with other employment records under Irish employment law. Usafety stores assessment history indefinitely, so there's no need to archive or purge for compliance reasons.
